Anesthesia and Cognitive Impairment

A Comprehensive Review of Perioperative Neurocognitive Disorders

Introduction

Advances in anesthetic techniques have transformed modern surgery, enabling increasingly complex procedures in older and medically fragile patients. Alongside these benefits, concern has grown regarding the effects of anesthesia and surgery on brain health. Cognitive changes following anesthesia are now recognized as a significant clinical and public health issue [1].

Patients and families frequently report problems with memory, attention, executive function, and mental clarity after surgery. While many of these symptoms are transient, a subset of patients experience persistent or even progressive cognitive decline [5].

Evolution of Terminology

Historically, postoperative cognitive changes were grouped under the term postoperative cognitive dysfunction (POCD). However, inconsistencies in definitions and diagnostic criteria limited clinical applicability.

In 2018, an international working group proposed the umbrella term Perioperative Neurocognitive Disorders (PND) to align perioperative cognitive research with DSM-5 nomenclature [1].

Current Classification

  • Postoperative Delirium (POD): Acute, fluctuating disturbance of attention and awareness [2]
  • Delayed Neurocognitive Recovery: Cognitive decline up to 30 days post-surgery
  • Postoperative Neurocognitive Disorder: Persistent impairment beyond 30 days [6]

Postoperative Delirium

Postoperative delirium is the most common acute neuropsychiatric complication after surgery, particularly in elderly and critically ill patients [2]. It is characterized by fluctuating consciousness, disorganized thinking, and inattention.

Delirium is associated with prolonged hospital stay, increased morbidity, long-term cognitive decline, and mortality [4].

Postoperative Cognitive Dysfunction

POCD refers to subtle impairments in memory, processing speed, and executive function detected through neuropsychological testing. Unlike delirium, POCD lacks overt clinical features and often goes unrecognized [3].

Large cohort studies have demonstrated that POCD may persist for months or years and is associated with reduced quality of life and increased dependency [6].

Pathophysiological Mechanisms

Neuroinflammation

Surgical trauma triggers a systemic inflammatory response that may extend to the central nervous system. Peripheral cytokines can disrupt the blood–brain barrier, activate microglia, and impair synaptic plasticity [8].

Neurotransmitter Imbalance

Anesthetic agents influence cholinergic, GABAergic, and glutamatergic pathways. Reduced acetylcholine activity has been strongly implicated in delirium [4].

Neurodegenerative Vulnerability

Patients with preclinical Alzheimer’s disease may exhibit exaggerated cognitive decline after anesthesia and surgery, suggesting a “second-hit” phenomenon [9].

Role of Anesthetic Technique

Evidence comparing regional and general anesthesia remains mixed. While regional techniques may reduce delirium risk in some populations, surgical stress itself appears to be a dominant factor [7].

Depth of anesthesia monitoring and avoidance of excessive anesthetic dosing have shown promise in reducing delirium incidence [10].

Prevention and Mitigation Strategies

  • Preoperative cognitive screening
  • Optimization of pain control
  • Early mobilization and sleep preservation
  • Judicious use of sedatives and anticholinergics

Clinical and Educational Implications

Understanding perioperative neurocognitive disorders is essential for anesthesiology trainees, surgeons, and perioperative teams. Clear communication with patients regarding cognitive risks is increasingly considered part of informed consent.

Conclusion

Cognitive impairment following anesthesia and surgery represents a complex interplay between patient vulnerability, surgical stress, and neurobiological mechanisms. Ongoing research and multidisciplinary approaches are essential to improve outcomes and preserve brain health across the lifespan.
